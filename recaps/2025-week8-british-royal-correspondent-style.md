Still, a 150.5-point showing is nothing to scoff at—many a lesser lord would gladly accept such production.

The implications of this defeat resonate throughout Lord Chris's season campaign. While he remains competitive in the grand scheme of the league's hierarchy, this loss to Lord Joe serves as a reminder that in fantasy football, as in matters of state, one must bring one's absolute finest performance when facing the league's elite. Perhaps a spot of tea and strategic contemplation are in order for next week's preparations.

## **THE BOURGEOIS STRUGGLE: MITCH VS. MATT**

*When Commoners Collide in Mediocrity Most Profound*

In what can only be described as a rather pedestrian affair more suited to a village cricket match than the grand stages of fantasy football, Sir Mitch and Sir Matt engaged in combat that resulted in the narrowest of victories for Matt's estate, 82.0 to 80.5 net points. One hesitates to use the word "victory" for such a display—perhaps "survival" would be more appropriate, rather like claiming triumph for finishing one's Brussels sprouts at dinner.

Sir Matt's modest victory was anchored by the workmanlike efforts of Derrick Henry and C.J. Stroud, each contributing 19 points with all the excitement of watching paint dry on the palace walls. Michael Pittman Jr. added 14 points, a performance that, while technically proficient, lacked the theatrical flair one expects from wide receivers named after nobility. The absence of several key performers due to injury—including A.J. Brown, Bryce Young, and J.J. McCarthy—left Matt's lineup looking rather like a state dinner with half the guests calling in sick with the vapors.

Poor Sir Mitch, meanwhile, suffered through what can only be described as a fantasy football nightmare worthy of Shakespeare's darkest tragedies. With Lamar Jackson, Brock Purdy, and Michael Penix Jr. all sidelined with various maladies, his quarterback situation resembled the Tower of London—full of broken dreams and abandoned hope. Kareem Hunt's 16 points and Samaje Perine's 14 points represented valiant efforts, but with Puka Nacua and Cam Skattebo also joining the injury ward, Mitch's roster looked more like a field hospital after Waterloo than a competitive fantasy squad.

The 1.5-point margin of defeat must sting particularly sharply for Sir Mitch, knowing that any number of small decisions could have swung this contest in his favor. Both teams' sub-90 point performances serve as a stark reminder that in fantasy football, as in life, sometimes one must simply endure and hope for better days ahead. Neither owner should expect an invitation to the royal box after such displays.

Lord Bruce's quarterback triumvirate performed like a perfectly choreographed changing of the guard ceremony, with Drake Maye leading the charge at 27 points, followed closely by Bo Nix's 26 points and Daniel Jones's surprising 24-point contribution. This three-headed hydra of quarterback excellence produced 77 points from the position alone—more than many teams managed in their entirety! The strategic deployment of multiple quarterbacks by Lord Bruce represents the kind of innovative thinking that would make the Royal Military Academy proud.
